Globalisation in Pre-Modern World

Globalisation existed long before the modern age, though it was slower and less connected than today. Ancient kingdoms and empires traded goods, exchanged ideas, and spread culture across continents.

  • India’s spices, textiles, and precious stones were famous in Rome and Southeast Asia.
  • The spread of religions like Buddhism and Islam illustrates cultural globalisation.
  • Empires such as the Roman, Mauryan, and Mongol empires built vast trade routes.
  • Caravans, ships, and migration created connections despite lack of modern transport.

Silk Route and Indentured Labour

The Silk Route was a vital trade network connecting China, India, Central Asia, and Europe. Goods like silk, spices, and cotton moved along these routes, as did ideas and diseases like the plague.

  • India was both a producer and a transit hub along Silk Route.
  • During colonial times, Indian indentured labourers worked in British colonies spreading culture worldwide.
  • They faced harsh contracts but helped globalise Indian food, traditions, and customs.

Economic Globalisation (1991 Liberalisation)

India’s economic globalisation took off in 1991 with liberalisation reforms opening the economy to foreign investment and trade.

  • Reduction of tariffs and trade barriers improved market access.
  • IT companies like Infosys and TCS grew rapidly serving global clients.
  • Foreign direct investment boosted multiple sectors—automobiles, telecom, retail.

Role of Transport and Communication

Modern transport and communication technologies are key drivers of globalisation, making movement faster and connecting people instantly.

  • Container ships, airplanes, and railways enable global cargo movement.
  • Mobile phones, satellites, and the internet allow real-time global communication.
  • Indian professionals work remotely with international companies.

Positive and Negative Impact of Globalisation on India

Globalisation has brought many benefits like job creation and foreign investment, but also challenges for small producers and local workers.

  • Positive: Foreign investment, IT growth, consumer choices, cultural spread, tourism.
  • Negative: Small producers undercut, job losses in traditional sectors, growing inequality.

Role of WTO in Promoting Globalisation

The World Trade Organization (WTO), established in 1995, encourages free trade and settles disputes, offering India access to global markets.

  • WTO promotes reduction in import duties and market opening.
  • Policies can favour developed countries, creating inequality challenges.

Challenges in Fair Globalisation

Ensuring fair wages, safe working conditions, environmental protection, and balanced benefits remain major challenges for India.

  • Need for skill training and support for small industries
  • Stronger international agreements to protect weaker economies
  • Inclusive growth and environmental sustainability

Regional Impact

Tamil Nadu thrives in textiles, automobiles, IT exports, and tourism thanks to globalisation. However, small weavers face challenges from cheap imports.

Exports of handicrafts and tourism have increased, but machine-made competition and western cultural changes affect local artisans.
